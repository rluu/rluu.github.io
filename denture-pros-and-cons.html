Denture Approaches: Pros and Cons

Traditional Removable Dentures (No Implants)

Pros

  • Least expensive option
  • No surgery required
  • Fastest to get (can often be fitted in a few visits)
  • Easy to repair or replace
  • Good option for patients who can't undergo surgery due to health conditions or insufficient jawbone

Cons

  • Can slip, shift, or click while eating and talking
  • Often requires adhesive paste for stability
  • Covers the palate (upper denture), which can reduce taste sensation and feel bulky
  • Jawbone continues to shrink over time (bone resorption) since there are no implant posts stimulating the bone
  • Need periodic relining/adjustment as the jawbone changes shape
  • Lower chewing efficiency (roughly 25-40% of natural teeth)
  • Can cause sore spots on gums

Implant-Retained Removable Dentures (Snap-On)

Pros

  • Much more stable than traditional dentures - no slipping
  • No adhesive paste needed
  • Implant posts help slow jawbone resorption
  • Upper denture can be designed without covering the full palate, improving comfort and taste
  • Removable for easy cleaning
  • Fewer implants needed (typically 2-4 per arch), making it less costly than fixed
  • Better chewing efficiency than traditional dentures

Cons

  • Requires surgery to place the implant posts
  • More expensive than traditional dentures
  • Attachments (locators, clips, or bar) wear over time and need periodic replacement
  • The denture itself still needs replacement roughly every 10-15 years
  • Healing time of 3-6 months after implant placement before the denture can be attached
  • Still not as stable as fixed implant dentures
  • Requires adequate jawbone density (or bone grafting beforehand)

Fixed Implant Dentures (All-on-4, All-on-6)

Pros

  • Most stable and secure option - closest to natural teeth
  • Highest chewing efficiency (roughly 80-90% of natural teeth)
  • Does not need to be removed daily
  • No palate coverage - feels most natural
  • Best at preserving jawbone due to more implant posts stimulating the bone
  • Most natural appearance (less plastic/acrylic visible)
  • Typically lasts the longest

Cons

  • Most expensive option (often $15,000-$30,000+ per arch)
  • Most invasive surgery (4-6+ implants per arch)
  • Harder to clean - requires water flossers, special brushes, and regular professional cleanings
  • Can only be removed by a dentist for deep cleaning or repairs
  • Longer treatment timeline if bone grafting is needed
  • If a component breaks, repair can be more complex and costly
  • Not suitable for all patients (requires sufficient bone density and good overall health)

The right choice depends on budget, oral health, jawbone condition, and personal preference. Most dental practices offer consultations to assess which approach is best suited for your situation.
